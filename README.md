## Reflection

### Interpreting User Needs and User Stories
Interpreting user needs starts with understanding what the user is actually trying to accomplish, not just what they are asking for on the surface. One thing I learned in this course is that user stories help break that down into something more manageable. Instead of focusing on technical requirements first, user stories focus on the user’s perspective and what value they are trying to get. This makes it easier to design features that actually solve real problems. User stories also help keep development organized because they break large ideas into smaller, actionable pieces that can be worked on during sprints.

### My Approach to Developing Programs and Agile Practices
My approach to developing programs has changed over this course. Before, I would have thought more in terms of just building something from start to finish. Now I understand the importance of breaking work into smaller pieces, testing often, and adapting as needed. Agile practices like sprint planning, daily standups, and retrospectives help keep development on track and allow teams to adjust when things change. In the future, I want to continue using these Agile practices because they help improve both the quality of the work and the efficiency of the process. I also think focusing on continuous improvement is important, especially in a field like software development where things are always evolving.

### Being a Good Team Member in Software Development
Being a good team member in software development means being able to communicate clearly, work well with others, and stay open to feedback. This course showed me that software development is not just about writing code, but about working as part of a team to deliver value. A good team member is someone who contributes, supports others, and is willing to adapt when needed. Communication is especially important because without it, teams can easily run into problems like duplicate work or missed requirements. Overall, being a good team member means understanding your role, staying accountable, and helping the team succeed as a whole.
